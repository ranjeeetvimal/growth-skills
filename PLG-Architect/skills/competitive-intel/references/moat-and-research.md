# Competitive Research + Moat Patterns

## Auto-find competitors (do NOT ask the founder to list them)
From the product + category in founder-context, run web searches yourself:
- `"[category] tools 2026"`, `"best [category] software"`, `"[category] alternatives"`
- `"[known incumbent] alternatives"`, `"[incumbent] vs"`, `"[incumbent] pricing"`
- `"[category] for [ICP]"` (niche-specific players)
Aim for **8-12 real competitors**. Cross-check pricing/free-tier claims across ≥2
sources (vendor sites often differ from review sites). Note the retrieval date and
that some sources are competitor blogs (biased). Then ask the founder ONE thing:
"Here's who I found — who do you actually lose deals to, and did I miss anyone?"

## Competitor profile table
| # | Competitor | Pricing model | Free tier | Positioning | Weakness you exploit |
Keep each cell short. The last column is the point — where you win.

## Positioning matrix
Pick the two axes that actually separate players (e.g. metered↔flat,
horizontal↔niche). Plot everyone. Find the empty/uncontested quadrant. Name the
axis nobody else claims (often: a fear, a job, or a buyer everyone ignores).

## Moat test (run on every claimed advantage)
Copyable in <1 week = not a moat. <3 months = feature, not moat. 1yr+ = real.
- **Fake moats:** features (weeks), pricing (days), UI (months), generic "compliance/safety" claims everyone makes.
- **Real moats:** switching costs, data network effects, counter-positioning
  (something the incumbent can't copy without hurting itself), brand/community
  density in a niche, regulatory/integration lock-in earned over time.

## Making a table-stakes claim into a moat
"Safe" or "cheap" alone is copyable. It becomes defensible only when you make it
the *architecture* (demonstrable product mechanics + the most-cited resource in
the niche + a community that trusts you for it) — not a tagline.

## What-if scenarios (3, with hedges)
1. Closest competitor copies your best feature — likelihood/impact/hedge.
2. A well-funded entrant arrives — likelihood/impact/hedge.
3. The incumbent changes strategy/price — likelihood/impact/hedge.

## Opportunity-spotting
The best openings are incumbent stumbles: a price hike, a gutted free plan, an
upmarket drift that abandons small users. These are time-boxed — name the window.
